Design by the Decade:
What the Fortune 500 Tells Us books the Best Logos of All Time

Explore the history of corporate logo design since the 1950s

By Kyra Moran | December 2022

Corporate logos are all around us. In fact, it is estimated that Americans see upwards of 4,000 logos in a single day. Covering a wide range of media including television, billboards, websites, magazines, and social media, it is almost impossible to avoid the influence of corporate branding. So, what makes a good logo? Why have some logos stood the test of time while others have faded into the past?

In this story, I gathered the Fortune 500 lists from every year since 1955, when the first Fortune 500 list was published. Given that these lists include the most profitable American companies from each year, it is fair to say that these companies have made a name for themselves by creating a brand identity, which of course includes a good logo. However, as you will see, corporate logo design changes with the times, and many companies rebrand in order for their logos to remain relevant. By examining the design trends of each decade and how logos have changed over time, maybe we can find a formula for the "perfect" corporate logo. Let's dive in.

During WWII, Americans had put all efforts towards the war, bringing American production and manufacturing to a halt. In the 1950s, Americans were flush with cash and companies expanded production, bringing books an unprecedented era of American consumerism. The rapid development of technology made it harder for companies to beat their competition with high-quality products alone, so this decade forced companies to distinguish themselves by creating a brand identity and reputation. With this rise in technology, television was also becoming a staple in the American household, and companies saw this as an opportunity for advertising.

The logos of the 1950s represent Modernism with sans-serif type, primary colors, and simple, geometric forms or symbols. Design was simplified through the integration of text and image into overlapping forms. American idealism in this decade led to experimentation in logo design, as designers sought to create logos that could work in various types of media and that could be recognized more quickly as the world picked up its pace after the war. Despite the rise in television, print media was still the primary source of information for Americans, so many logos remained without gradients, shadows, or intricate details that would be hard to print.

In the 1960s, branding became a necessity for companies. Many of the logos built upon the simplicity of 1950s logos, further reducing ornamentation and sticking to basic sans-serif typefaces and geometric shapes. The further integration of television in American society and the development of direct marketing led to companies bringing more color into their logo designs to appeal to consumers.

The 2000s brought books the introduction of the first IPhone in 2007 and the App Store in 2008. Logos now needed to fit in a 64 by 64 pixel square to be accessible to smartphone users. As the world became more comfortable with digital technologies, it was no longer necessary to mimic a three-dimensional world in a two-dimensional space. This began the era of flat design that has continued into the present. The 2000s also saw rounded letters and brighter colors.

In the present day, there are more brands than ever before and branding has become more important than the product itself in distinguishing a company from its competitors. With Americans being surrounded by a variety of physical and digital media, logos have to be incredibly flexible and adaptable, while getting across a message in a short period of time.

After looking at eight decades of the top corporate logos, what can we conclude? Is there anything that stood the test of time? Almost every decade was filled with sans-serif typefaces, basic geometric shapes, and minimal effects. The colors red and blue were the most popular in every decade, and no, this is not because of their patriotism. Rather, red is the most visible color to the human eye and blue is representative of trust, loyalty, confidence, and intelligence while also having a calming effect. More so than colors, typefaces, and shapes, it seems that the technologies and media forms at a given time are the largest drivers of logo design. The logos that stood the test of time were flexible enough to be printed or displayed digitally and could look good at any scale. In a fast pace world, people don't have time to examine the details, so simplicity is key.

This leaves us with our formula for the perfect logo:

Sans-Serif Typeface + Red & Blue + Flexibility + Scalability + Simplicity
